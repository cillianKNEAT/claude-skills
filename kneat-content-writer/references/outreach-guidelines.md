# Kneat Outreach Copy Guidelines (LinkedIn / Email)

These guidelines apply on top of the core editorial standards (`editorial-standards.md`) whenever the content is a LinkedIn message or outreach email — sales outreach, follow-ups, or any other prospect-facing message. Where these rules and the general editorial standards overlap (e.g., conciseness, tone), the rules here take precedence for outreach copy specifically, since outreach has a different job than long-form content.

## General Style (applies to every outreach message)

- **Length**: concise and easy to scan — maximum 200 words unless the user states otherwise
- **No filler words**: cut "just," "only," "really," "actually," and similar hedges/softeners
- **Direct, clear, efficient**: one idea per sentence, no throat-clearing
- **Do not over-explain**: state the point once, then move on — do not restate or justify at length
- **Tone**: professional but conversational — write like a knowledgeable person, not a brochure
- **Recipient-focused**: every message should center the recipient's role, priorities, and likely challenges, not Kneat's product or agenda

## Sales Outreach (first-touch and early messages)

- **No generic introductions**: never open with "I wanted to introduce Kneat," "My name is X and I work at Kneat," or similar throat-clearing
- **Clear reason for reaching out**: the opening line must make it obvious why *this person, in this role* is being contacted
- **Lead with the problem**: open with a relevant business problem or industry challenge the recipient likely faces — not with Kneat or its features
- **Connect to measurable business impact**: tie the challenge to a concrete cost — time, cost, compliance risk, speed to market, audit readiness, or similar — not just the problem in the abstract
- **Structure**: problem → solution → conversation
  1. Name a specific, credible challenge tied to the recipient's role or industry, and connect it to measurable impact
  2. Point to how Kneat's approach addresses it (briefly — do not pitch the full feature set) — mention Kneat only after the challenge and its relevance have been established, never in the opening line
  3. Invite a conversation with a low-friction ask (a question, a short call, a resource)
- **Educate, do not sell**: the message should teach the reader something about their own problem space, not push product
- **Proof points**: where it strengthens the message, include one specific proof point — a customer example, a measurable outcome, or an industry trend. One is enough; do not stack statistics or stack multiple proof points in a single message
- **Avoid marketing-pitch tone**: no superlatives ("industry-leading," "revolutionary"), no exclamation points, no stacked benefit claims
- **Subject line** (email only): short (3–5 words), relevant to the message, and curiosity-driven — never clickbait

## Call to Action

- **Do not ask for time in the first email or message** (no "worth 15 minutes?" on a first touch) — earn the conversation before asking for a meeting
- **Prefer a genuine, low-commitment question** that invites a reply without asking the reader to schedule anything, for example:
  - "How are you currently approaching validation documentation across your sites?"
  - "Is this something you're currently evaluating?"
- **One CTA per message**: never stack a question and a meeting ask, or two questions, in the same message
- Save a direct ask for time (a call, a demo) for later in the sequence, once the reader has engaged

## LinkedIn Outreach

- **Shorter than email**: LinkedIn messages should be even more compressed than an outreach email — trim to the essential problem statement and one CTA
- **Tone**: slightly more conversational than email while remaining professional — LinkedIn is a lower-formality channel, but the same no-filler, no-pitch rules still apply

## Follow-Up Emails

- **Very short**: 2–4 lines total, no exceptions
- **Do not repeat the value proposition**: assume the reader already saw it in the first message
- **Do not restate product details**: no re-explaining what Kneat does or how it works
- **Tone**: a gentle reminder, not a second pitch
- **Ending**: always close with one simple next step or question (e.g., "Worth a quick call this week?" or "Should I send over the case study?")

## Things to Avoid (all outreach copy)

- Opening with "Hope you are doing well" or similar generic pleasantries
- "Checking in," "circling back," "just following up," or other filler framing for follow-ups
- Assuming the prospect's priorities or initiatives without evidence — see AI Guidance below
- Asking more than one question in a single message
- Including more than one clear CTA in a single message

## AI Guidance

- **Never invent company initiatives, projects, or business challenges.** Personalization must be grounded in information the user provided or publicly available facts — do not fabricate a specific detail to make a message feel personalized
- **If there isn't enough information to personalize**, write a high-quality, industry-specific message instead of inventing detail to fill the gap — a strong generic message beats a fabricated personal one
- **Every sentence should have a purpose.** Remove anything that doesn't move the conversation forward — if a line doesn't add new information or advance the ask, cut it

## Quick Self-Check for Outreach Copy

Before delivering any outreach message, confirm:

- [ ] Word count within limit (200 words for first-touch, or as specified; 2–4 lines for follow-ups; LinkedIn messages shorter than the email equivalent)
- [ ] No filler words
- [ ] Opens with the recipient's problem or context, not a Kneat introduction, and not "Hope you are doing well" or similar pleasantries
- [ ] Challenge is connected to a measurable business impact (time, cost, compliance risk, speed to market, audit readiness, etc.)
- [ ] Kneat is mentioned only after the challenge and its relevance are established — not in the opening line
- [ ] At most one proof point (customer example, measurable outcome, or industry trend) — not stacked statistics
- [ ] Subject line (if email) is 3–5 words, relevant, and curiosity-driven — not clickbait
- [ ] Exactly one CTA, and it is a single question — not stacked questions, and not a meeting/time ask if this is a first-touch message
- [ ] No "checking in," "circling back," or "just following up" language (follow-ups)
- [ ] No restated value proposition (if this is a follow-up)
- [ ] No assumptions about the prospect's priorities or initiatives that aren't grounded in provided or public information
- [ ] Reads as educational/conversational, not as a pitch
- [ ] Every sentence has a clear purpose — nothing that doesn't move the conversation forward
- [ ] Still complies with core editorial standards (spelling, Oxford comma, em-dash formatting, etc. — these still apply even in short-form copy)
